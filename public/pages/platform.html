Empowering You

The USC's ultimate goal is enhancing the student experience. Team Litchfield believes that the best way to achieve this is though empowering you. Empowerment means providing high caliber programming, comprehensive support, and enabling students to pursue their goals.

Mental Health

The first step to empowerment is a healthy mind. Team Litchfield will make mental health a priority. We have already discussed all the ways we will lobby for better mental health support. We also have many direct action plans to improve mental health services for Western students.

• The Jack Project – jack.org

Named after former Queens student Jack Windeler, the Jack Project is a program dedicated to promoting mental health awareness for university students. We will reach out to this group to collaborate on awareness campaigns to normalize issues and empower people to seek help.

• Student Assistance Program

A student assistance program provides online, phone, and app based mental health resources and counseling to students. Team Litchfield has consulted Aspiria.org to investigate the feasibility of setting up a student assistance program on campus. To support this initiative we would work with the university and provincial government to apply for mental health grants. We would also look into including this service in the current student health plan. With potential opt-in for students with other coverage. Aspiria provides psychiatric, financial, life, and legal counseling for students. It would also be integrated with existing mental health services to provide comprehensive support. The program would prioritize directing students to existing services when available to ensure that university services are not undermined. Team Litchfield will work with the new Associate Vice-President Student Experience to deliver a student assistance program to Western. We are confident this will fundamentally chance the way Western students access mental health support, drastically reduce our service availability problems, and cut down unacceptable wait times. If a student assistance program is brought to campus they are committed to helping the USC market their services to ensure all students are aware of their new services.

• Centralized Mental Health Directory

There are so many different support services on campus. We will create a consolidated and easily accessible database of these services. This will work with the student assistance program to make services more accessible to students and increase their awareness of them.

• De-Stressor Programming

Another way to reduce mental health problems on campus is to alleviate the causes. We will increase our popular de-stressor programming and expand its reach outside of exam periods. We will also encourage councils to collaborate on de-stressor programming to give targeted relief to their own constituencies. We will also push for more reviews and reforms of academic counseling to reduce unnecessary academic stresses. The amount of students forced to seek accommodations for academic related anxiety is far too high. This will be paired with seminars on test preparation and time management to help further reduce stress.

• Mental Health First Aid Training

Mental health crises can occur at anytime and anywhere. We firmly believe in providing mental health first aid training to people likely to be approached by students in crisis.

Staff and Faculty

• Team Litchfield will work with the new Western AVPSE to get mental health first aid training to professors, TAs, and staff members

Student Leaders

• We will work with the Western AVPSE and Orientation Planning Committee to provide mental health first aid training to Sophs and council members

Mental health first aid training will equip likely responders to support students in crisis and direct them safely to appropriate resources. It will also promote a higher level of understanding for services available on campus and in the community

• Partnering With Community Support

There are community group like Connect running mental health support and peer-to-peer counseling on campus. The USC should partner with the groups to increase the services available on campus. We should also help these groups promote their services.

• No Wrong Door Policy

The “no wrong door” policy requires any leader, staff member, volunteer, or administrator - who is contacted by a student about a concern - to take it upon themselves to send that student to the right place to have their concern addressed. This idea has already been introduced at the USC but it needs to be implemented and supported by the university. Team Litchfield will push for this.

• Soft Transition Policy

The “soft transition” policy supports the “no wrong door” policy by requiring staff, leaders, volunteers, and administrators to tell students where to go and follow up to make sure they get there. This means picking up the phone and calling ahead for them. It can also mean physically walking them to the other office. We believe this policy is a very important addition to student-staff interactions and that it will help combat confusing services and awareness issues. Most students only take the initiative to reach out once. We want to make sure they get the help they need.
